## Overview

Altamaha-ha is an aquatic cryptid associated with the lower Altamaha River, the Altamaha Sound, and the surrounding marshes of McIntosh County, Georgia. The creature has roots in Muscogee (Creek) oral tradition and has been a subject of local folklore in the town of Darien for generations. Modern accounts describe a long-bodied, serpentine animal with a horse- or alligator-like head and flippers, often nicknamed 'Altie'. Like the Loch Ness Monster and Ogopogo, the creature is part of a wider tradition of freshwater / brackish lake-and-river monsters and there is no scientifically accepted evidence of its existence. A 2018 'carcass' photographed on Wolf Island National Wildlife Refuge was later revealed by performance artist Zardulu to be a fabrication built from a stuffed shark and papier-mâché. Skeptical commentators have proposed misidentification of large alligator gar, sturgeon, or manatees as parsimonious explanations for sightings.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: The name comes from the Altamaha River, formed by the confluence of the Oconee and Ocmulgee in southeastern Georgia. The legend has roots in Muscogee (Creek) tradition.
- **Meaning**: The river name 'Altamaha' itself is of Indigenous origin; '-ha' has been popularly glossed by local users as a creature suffix, but no academic etymology endorses the gloss.
- **First use**: The name 'Altamaha-ha' enters print in late-20th-century Georgia local history and tourist literature.

## Notable Sightings

### Wolf Island 'Altie carcass' ⭐
> **Date**: 2018-08-15  
> **Location**: Beach of Wolf Island National Wildlife Refuge, mouth of the Altamaha River  
Photographs of a serpentine 'carcass' on the beach of Wolf Island circulated widely online and in regional news. The animal was claimed by some witnesses as evidence of Altamaha-ha.
